Your palm print should belong to you. Once you register your palm print with an Amazon One device, it is stored in the Amazon Web Services (AWS) cloud. Amazon considers the act of scanning your palm “informed consent” to do so. This cloud-storing approach is different from the way that other companies store biometric information. For example, Apple stores your face ID and fingerprint inside a chip in your phone. Do you trust Amazon to keep your data safe?

Even without the threat of hackers, do you trust Amazon itself as a corporation? Your palm print is unique to you, and with it, Amazon could potentially identify you in ways you don’t want them to. Giving them your palm print is like getting a hand tattoo of your social security number in an ink that only Amazon’s technology can read. With a few legal loopholes, they could likely sell pictures of your tattoo to others.

Your body is not a vessel for consumption. You may feel like there’s no point in keeping your privacy anymore; think about how corporations like Meta already have your online data and use it to sell you ideas and products. However, these companies are just using your online footprint, not your body. Instead of paying with a device, paying with Amazon One makes your body itself into a wallet. Although these large corporations see you only as a number preceded with a dollar sign, you are much more.
